The Supreme Court of India has put on hold its November 20 ruling that limited the definition of the Aravalli range to landforms rising more than 100 metres above the surrounding terrain. The court has decided to reopen the matter for a comprehensive review.

A bench led by Chief Justice of India Surya Kant, along with Justices J.K. Maheshwari and A.G. Masih, placed the earlier judgment ‚Äúin abeyance‚Äù and indicated the need for expert inputs to ensure a balanced and scientifically sound approach.

The court proposed the formation of a high-level expert committee to study ecological continuity, assess the scientific basis of the height-based definition, and examine the regulation of mining activities across the Aravalli region.

The issue was taken up suo motu on December 27 after the earlier ruling attracted criticism from environmental experts and stakeholders. During the hearing, the bench observed that an independent and impartial expert opinion was essential to address unresolved concerns.

Solicitor General Tushar Mehta, appearing for the Union government, welcomed the court‚Äôs intervention and noted that several misunderstandings had emerged following the November order. He pointed out that the ruling had relied on a court-appointed expert report and had envisaged a detailed mining plan subject to Supreme Court approval.

The bench expressed concern that restricting the Aravallis to elevations above 100 metres could reduce protected areas and create regulatory gaps. It questioned how mining would be governed in zones lying between qualifying hill formations, warning that such an approach could fragment a continuous ecological system.

Emphasising the need to preserve the geological and ecological integrity of the ancient mountain range, the court suggested that a broader environmental assessment may be necessary to avoid weakening existing safeguards.

At the request of the Solicitor General, the court directed the Union government to submit its response before finalising the composition of the expert committee. Notices have been issued to the Centre, concerned states and the amicus curiae. The matter will be heard next on January 21, 2026.

The interim stay is expected to pause the mapping and delineation exercise initiated by the Union environment ministry and state governments, which was based on the now-suspended elevation criterion.

Stretching over 700 kilometres from Gujarat to Delhi through Rajasthan and Haryana, the Aravalli range plays a crucial role in preventing desertification, replenishing groundwater and sustaining biodiversity across northern and western India.
